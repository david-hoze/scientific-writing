# Local-to-global structure in circuit complexity: recent frontiers

**The period 2022–2026 has produced breakthrough results connecting local and global circuit complexity, with conditional NP-hardness of MCSP achieved, a new uniform magnification framework that appears to sidestep the locality barrier, and a definitive proof that the approximation method inherently localizes.** These results reshape the landscape of proof barriers and meta-complexity, though the central open problems—unconditional superpolynomial circuit lower bounds and unconditional NP-hardness of MCSP—remain unresolved. The emerging picture is that local-to-global phenomena pervade circuit complexity at every level, from restriction-based lower bounds through self-reducibility amplification to hardness magnification, yet no current technique fully exploits this structure to prove strong lower bounds for general circuits.

## Restriction complexity and the trivial local-global gap

The question of whether a Boolean function can have all small-subcube restrictions efficiently computable while its global circuit complexity is large has a trivially positive answer—but the interesting versions remain wide open. For any function f : {0,1}^n → {0,1} with circuit complexity S(n), every restriction to a k-dimensional subcube has complexity at most **O(2^k / k)** by Shannon's counting argument. Setting k = c·log n yields polynomial local complexity regardless of how large S(n) is. This means every conjectured-hard function (e.g., SAT's characteristic function restricted to inputs of fixed length) already exhibits a superpolynomial local-global gap on O(log n)-dimensional subcubes.

The non-trivial question is at what subcube dimension local complexity must begin reflecting global complexity. Gate elimination—the only technique yielding general circuit lower bounds—provides a partial answer. The Find–Golovnev–Hirsch–Kulikov result (FOCS 2016) proving a **3.01n** lower bound over the full binary basis B₂ works by showing that *some* single-variable restriction eliminates at least 3 gates. By induction, some restriction to an (n−k)-dimensional subcube reduces circuit size by Ω(k). This guarantees the *existence* of complex restrictions but says nothing about whether *all* restrictions can simultaneously be simple.

For formulas (fan-out-one circuits), restriction methods give precise quantitative control. The Håstad–Tal shrinkage theorem establishes that under a p-random restriction, a formula of size S shrinks to expected size **O(p² · S)**—the shrinkage exponent is exactly 2. The Andreev function achieves the current best formula lower bound of Ω̃(n³), and its restrictions to √n-dimensional subcubes still require non-trivial formulas. For constant-depth circuits (AC⁰), Håstad's switching lemma gives exponential lower bounds for PARITY, and these propagate smoothly to restrictions: PARITY_k requires depth-(d+1) AC⁰ circuits of size exp(Ω(k^{1/d})), so there is essentially *no* local-global gap for PARITY in AC⁰.

**No explicit superpolynomial gap between restriction complexity and global complexity is known for general circuits**, because we cannot prove superlinear general circuit lower bounds. Recent developments that push the envelope include Wu's nearly-4log n depth lower bound for formulas with restricted top layers (2024, extending Mihajlin–Sofronova CCC 2022), Göös–Riazanov–Sofronova–Sokolov's top-down lower bounds for depth-4 circuits (FOCS 2023), and the Limaye–Srinivasan–Tavenas breakthrough of superpolynomial lower bounds against low-depth algebraic circuits (FOCS 2021, JACM 2025). None achieves superlinear lower bounds for unrestricted Boolean circuits.

## Self-reducibility creates rigid local-to-global constraints

SAT's downward self-reducibility—the property that φ is satisfiable iff φ[x₁=0] or φ[x₁=1] is satisfiable—creates a binary tree of sub-instances where local decisions must propagate consistently to the global answer. This structure has concrete consequences for circuit complexity that go well beyond a mere conceptual analogy.

The most striking result is due to Allender and Koucký (JACM 2010): for many problems in NC¹ sharing a natural self-reducibility property, **there is no gap between barely-superlinear and polynomial circuit size in TC⁰**. Specifically, if such a problem has TC⁰ circuits of size n^{1+ε} for every ε > 0, it automatically has polynomial-size TC⁰ circuits. Boolean Formula Evaluation (BFE), being NC¹-complete, has this self-reducibility property. From known lower bounds (Impagliazzo–Paturi–Saks), BFE requires depth-d TC⁰ circuits of size n^{1+ε_d}; if one could make ε independent of depth, it would follow that **TC⁰ ≠ NC¹**. Williams (2013) extended this to CircEval, showing that sufficiently strong depth lower bounds would separate P from NC via the same self-reducibility mechanism.

Self-reducibility also underpins the Karp–Lipton theorem: if NP ⊆ P/poly, the polynomial hierarchy collapses to Σ₂ᵖ. The proof uses self-reducibility to verify purported circuits for SAT—local correctness at each step of the self-reduction guarantees global correctness, enabling a quantifier transposition from ∃∀ to ∀∃. This is precisely a local-to-global consistency argument where the "gluing condition" is provided by the self-reduction tree structure.

Harsha, Mitropolsky, and Rosen (ITCS 2023) formalized downward self-reducibility in the TFNP framework, proving that every downward self-reducible problem in TFNP lies in PLS. Notably, they showed the self-reducibility property is *not* preserved under general NP-complete reductions—it depends on input representation. A 2025 ECCC report (TR25-121) answered their open problem negatively: unless FP = PLS, some PLS-complete problems are not downward self-reducible.

Friedman's ambitious program (arXiv:cs/0512008, 2005) to model circuit depth via sheaf cohomology over Grothendieck topologies remains the most direct attempt to formalize the sheaf-theoretic analogy. He showed that AND gates correspond to bounded cohomological complexity via "virtual zero extensions" and negation gates preserve cohomological complexity via duality. However, achieving both bounds simultaneously—which would yield depth lower bounds—remains unresolved, and the program has not been substantially extended since 2006. Abramsky and Brandenburger's sheaf-theoretic contextuality framework (New J. Physics, 2011) precisely captures local-to-global consistency failures via non-vanishing Čech cohomology, but has not been applied to circuit lower bounds directly.

## MCSP hardness magnification enters a new phase

The hardness magnification program—showing that surprisingly weak lower bounds for meta-complexity problems would imply major separations—has produced its most significant developments in 2023–2025, with results on three major fronts: uniform magnification, conditional NP-hardness, and the OWF–meta-complexity equivalence.

**Atserias and Müller (arXiv:2503.24061, March 2025)** introduced "distinguishers"—sparse matrices retaining key properties of error-correcting codes—as a technically simple approach to magnification. Their main results are threefold. First, they showed that fixed-polynomial formula-size lower bounds for NP follow from slightly superlinear formula-size lower bounds for approximating any sufficiently sparse NP problem, with **sharp thresholds**. Second, and most significantly, they obtained a *uniform* magnification result for MCSP that "seems to sidestep the localization barrier." The locality barrier (Chen et al., JACM 2022) had been the primary obstruction to the magnification program because it showed that existing lower-bound techniques—which produce bounds valid even with small-fan-in oracle gates—cannot distinguish between the oracle circuits that magnification proves exist and the circuits the lower bound would need to rule out. Uniform magnification avoids this because the locality barrier relies on non-uniform oracle circuit constructions.

**Hirahara and Ilango (FOCS 2025)** achieved the first conditional NP-hardness result for MCSP on general Boolean circuits, addressing one of the oldest open questions in NP-completeness theory dating to Levin's 1973 work. Their main theorem states that a constant-factor approximation of MCSP is NP-hard under deterministic quasi-polynomial-time non-adaptive reductions, assuming three conditions:

- Subexponentially-secure non-interactive witness-indistinguishable proof systems (NIWIs) for SAT exist
- coNP requires subexponential-size nondeterministic circuits (coNP ⊄ i.o.NSIZE(2^{n^ε}))
- **P^{NP}/poly requires circuits of size δ · 2^n/n** almost everywhere for some constant δ > 0

This result circumvents all known barriers—Murray–Williams (ruling out n^{0.49}-time reductions), Hirahara–Watanabe (oracle-independent reductions), Mazor–Pass (Levin reductions in obfustopia), and Ko's oracle separations. All three assumptions are well-studied and plausible.

The Liu–Pass program connecting meta-complexity to one-way functions continued with major advances. Their foundational equivalence (FOCS 2020)—OWFs exist iff t-time-bounded Kolmogorov complexity K^t is mildly hard on average—was refined through a series of papers. At TCC 2024, they identified the first "OWF-complete" promise problem whose worst-case hardness characterizes OWFs. At CRYPTO 2025, they showed that under natural derandomization assumptions, worst-case hardness of "boundary" K^t (distinguishing K^t(x) ≥ n−1 from K^t(x) > n − log n) characterizes OWFs. Their ITCS 2026 paper extended this to randomized K^t.

Hirahara's STOC 2023 result provided a different characterization: **OWFs exist iff distributional Kolmogorov complexity is NP-hard (and NP is worst-case hard)**. This was complemented by the Chen–Hirahara–Ren result (STOC 2024) that functions in symmetric exponential time (S₂E) require near-maximum **2^n/n** circuit size, and Chen–Li–Liang's result (ECCC TR24-182) that AM^{EXP}_{/2^{n^ε}} requires circuits of size ≥ 2^n/n.

The Gap-MCSP front has seen important negative results alongside the magnification progress. Mazor and Pass (CCC 2024) showed that under standard cryptographic assumptions (iO + subexponentially-secure OWFs), Gap-MCSP and Gap-MKtP are **not NP-complete under Levin reductions**. Austrin and Risse (ECCC TR23-010) proved sum-of-squares requires degree Ω(s^{1−ε}) to certify that a function lacks circuits of size s, ruling out low-degree SoS proofs of NP ⊄ P/poly.

## PCPs of proximity bridge property testing and circuit lower bounds

The connection between property testing and circuit lower bounds is mediated primarily through PCPs of Proximity (PCPPs)—the property-testing formalization of probabilistically checkable proofs. Chen and Williams (CCC 2019) used PCPPs to sharpen the algorithmic approach to circuit lower bounds, showing that a non-trivial CAPP (Circuit Approximation Probability Problem) algorithm for XOR₂ ∘ C suffices to prove **NEXP ⊄ C** for any polynomial-size circuit class C closed under projections. Previously, Ben-Sasson and Viola (ICALP 2014) required CAPP for OR_{poly(n)} ∘ AND₃ ∘ C—a much stronger algorithmic assumption.

Williams' original proof that NEXP ⊄ ACC⁰ (J. ACM 2014) already used PCP reductions centrally: the PCP theorem converts a nondeterministic computation into a locally verifiable proof, and faster-than-brute-force SAT algorithms for ACC circuits then yield the lower bound via the nondeterministic time hierarchy. Chen, Lyu, and Williams (FOCS 2020) combined PCPP techniques with a new XOR Lemma to achieve *almost-everywhere* circuit lower bounds: **E^{NP}** is not (1/2 + 2^{−n^δ})-approximated by 2^{n^δ}-size ACC⁰ circuits for all sufficiently large n.

Kabanets and Impagliazzo's classical result (STOC 2003) directly connects a property testing problem to circuit lower bounds: if deterministic low-degree testing (checking whether a Boolean circuit computes a function close to a low-degree polynomial) is in P, then either NEXP ⊄ P/poly or the Permanent lacks polynomial arithmetic circuits. The construction of asymptotically good locally testable codes (c³-LTCs) by Dinur, Evra, Livne, Lubotzky, and Mozes (STOC 2022) resolved a longstanding open problem and further cemented the PCP–testing–coding connection. Recent work by Block et al. (arXiv:2602.20278, February 2026) proved the first exponential lower bound for 2-query relaxed locally decodable codes, exhibiting a phase transition.

Ferreira, Palit, and Raskhodnikova (ECCC TR25-185, November 2025) initiated a systematic study of *time complexity* (not just query complexity) of property testing, proving time-query hierarchy theorems and fine-grained lower bounds on tolerant testing for halfspaces conditional on the k-SUM conjecture. This represents the first provable separation between query and time complexity for natural testing problems.

## The locality barrier stands, but cracks are appearing

The Chen–Hirahara–Oliveira–Pich–Rajgopal–Santhanam locality barrier (ITCS 2020, JACM 2022) identifies a fundamental obstruction to the hardness magnification program. A lower-bound technique is **localizable** if it remains valid when circuits are augmented with arbitrarily powerful oracle gates of bounded fan-in. The barrier shows that magnification theorems always construct oracle circuits with small-fan-in gates for the target problem, while existing lower-bound techniques localize—so they cannot close the gap.

Pich (computational complexity 33, Article 12, 2024; arXiv:2212.09285) significantly strengthened this barrier by proving that **the entire approximation method is inherently localizable**. His main theorem shows: if the approximation complexity measure ρ_{O(d²)}(f, M) ≥ s for a depth-d circuit model M, then f cannot be computed by depth-d circuits of size s − poly(k·d·n·(2^{m/d}+1)) using k arbitrary oracles of arity m. For general circuits, the penalty is s − O(km²). This captures *all* AC⁰ lower bounds—Ajtai, Furst–Saxe–Sipser, Håstad, Razborov, Smolensky—as inherently local, making the barrier much more fundamental than initially understood.

Three potential routes around the barrier have emerged:

- **Uniform magnification**: Atserias–Müller's 2025 uniform magnification for MCSP appears to sidestep the locality barrier because the barrier depends on non-uniform oracle circuit constructions. However, no new unconditional lower bound has been derived from this approach yet.

- **Learning speedup**: Pich's result on learning algorithms from circuit lower bounds (computational complexity 34, Article 6, 2025; arXiv:2012.14095) explicitly notes that "a promising aspect of the learning speedup is that it avoids the locality barrier." This connects learning theory to magnification through a fundamentally different route.

- **Proof complexity**: Pich and Santhanam ("Towards P ≠ NP from Extended Frege Lower Bounds," accepted to JACM) showed P ≠ NP follows from superpolynomial lower bounds for Extended Frege under certain provability assumptions. Arteche, Khaniki, Pich, and Santhanam (ICALP 2024) showed that if implicit Extended Frege efficiently proves a standard derandomization assumption, any superpolynomial iEF lower bound implies **#P ⊄ FP/poly**. These proof-complexity approaches operate through propositional proof systems rather than combinatorial/algebraic arguments and are not subject to the locality barrier in the same way.

Chen, Hu, and Ren (ECCC TR25-184, ITCS 2026) established complementary *algebrization* barriers, showing that PostBPE ⊄ i.o.-SIZE[O(n)] requires non-algebrizing techniques. This reinforces the picture that multiple independent barriers constrain different proof strategies.

## The emerging synthesis and what remains open

The local-to-global theme unifies these disparate research threads more tightly than is typically appreciated. Self-reducibility amplification (Allender–Koucký), hardness magnification (Oliveira–Santhanam, Atserias–Müller), PCP local verification (Williams, Chen–Williams), and the locality barrier (Chen et al., Pich) all concern the same fundamental question: when can local circuit-complexity information determine global circuit-complexity behavior?

The most pressing open questions as of early 2026 are whether Atserias–Müller's uniform magnification can yield new unconditional lower bounds, whether the Hirahara–Ilango assumptions for MCSP NP-hardness can be weakened or removed, and whether the proof-complexity route can circumvent the locality barrier to produce new circuit lower bounds. The field has reached a point where the barriers themselves are well-characterized—locality for combinatorial methods, natural proofs for large properties, algebrization for simulation-based techniques—and the central challenge is to find techniques that simultaneously evade all three while remaining strong enough to prove lower bounds for the problems where magnification applies.
